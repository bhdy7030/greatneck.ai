CRITICAL INSPECTION TIMING — Don't-Miss Inspection Windows
============================================================
Knowledge Document for RAG Retrieval
Last Updated: 2026-03-04
Applies To: Village of Great Neck, Great Neck Estates, Great Neck Plaza, Kensington, Kings Point, Thomaston
Jurisdiction: Nassau County, New York — Town of North Hempstead
Governing Code: NYS Uniform Fire Prevention and Building Code (19 NYCRR Parts 1202-1203),
  2020 Residential Code of New York State (RCNYS), IRC R109 Inspections,
  2020 Energy Conservation Construction Code of New York State


PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT
=========================

Construction inspections exist to verify that work hidden behind walls, underground, or under
concrete actually meets code. Once work is concealed, the ONLY way to verify compliance is to
tear it back open — at enormous cost to the homeowner or contractor.

This document identifies every critical "don't-miss" inspection window: the exact moment when
work MUST be inspected BEFORE it gets covered, buried, or enclosed. Missing these windows
triggers mandatory exposure of concealed work — re-excavation, demolition of drywall, removal
of concrete — or results in stop-work orders, fines, and inability to obtain a Certificate of
Occupancy.

KEY LEGAL BASIS: Under 19 NYCRR 1203.3 and IRC R109, all concealed work — structural,
mechanical, electrical, plumbing — must be inspected and approved BEFORE concealment.
The building inspector must be given minimum 24-48 hours notice to schedule inspections.
No work may continue past an inspection point until that inspection is completed and approved.


=============================================================================
SECTION 1: DRIVEWAY, CURB CUT, AND SITE DRAINAGE WORK
=============================================================================

1.1 Drywell Inspection — BEFORE Backfill
------------------------------------------
WHAT: Stormwater drainage drywells must be inspected before they are buried.

WHEN EXACTLY: After the drywell structure is installed, the leaching collar (3-foot graded
  leaching material around the drywell per Nassau County drainage standards) is in place,
  and all connections are made — but BEFORE any backfill covers the drywell.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Drywell structure (precast rings or block construction)
  - 3-foot collar of graded leaching material
  - All inlet pipe connections
  - Overflow connections (if any)
  - Proper depth (max 25 feet per Nassau County standards; if suitable leaching material
    does not occur within 25 feet, excavation must extend 6 feet into suitable material)
  - Distance from structures (minimum 20 feet per Nassau County standards; may be reduced
    to 10 feet on lots of insufficient size with engineer certification per Town of North
    Hempstead code)
  - Temporary grate or weighted silt fence installed during construction per Town of
    North Hempstead requirement

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Complete re-excavation of drywell to expose connections and structure
  - Typical cost: $2,000-$5,000+ depending on depth and site conditions
  - Possible damage to newly paved driveway if drywell is under or adjacent to it
  - Cannot obtain CO without approved drainage inspection

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Nassau County DPW drainage standards govern drywell specifications
  - Town of North Hempstead requires drywells installed BEFORE clearing other areas for
    new construction
  - Village of Kings Point requires a separate drainage permit and drainage plan for all
    construction projects
  - Great Neck Estates surveys must show storm-water drywell locations

1.2 Subgrade Inspection — BEFORE Paving
-----------------------------------------
WHAT: The prepared base (subgrade) for driveway paving must be inspected before the
  concrete or asphalt is placed.

WHEN EXACTLY: After excavation is complete, base material (gravel/crushed stone) is spread
  and compacted, and any reinforcement (wire mesh or rebar for concrete driveways) is placed —
  but BEFORE concrete is poured or asphalt is laid.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Subgrade compaction and grading
  - Base material type and thickness
  - Proper drainage pitch (water must flow away from structures and not onto neighboring
    properties or public right-of-way)
  - Reinforcement placement (if concrete driveway)
  - Formwork for concrete
  - Driveway thickness compliance (Village of Great Neck: driveways within the street line
    must be at least 6 inches thick)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove and replace all paving to expose subgrade
  - Typical cost: $3,000-$10,000+ depending on driveway size and material
  - If drainage is wrong, may need to tear up and regrade entire area

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Village of Great Neck: All concrete must have minimum compressive strength of 3,300 psi
    at 28 days and must be air-entraining concrete
  - Village of Great Neck: Driveway design must be approved by the Superintendent of
    Public Works
  - Great Neck Estates: Permit required for any new driveway, patio, walkway, or paved
    area of 200 square feet or more (fee: $100 + $1/sq ft)

1.3 Curb Cut Forms Inspection — BEFORE Pour
---------------------------------------------
WHAT: Curb cut formwork must be inspected before concrete is placed.

WHEN EXACTLY: After forms are set for the curb cut, apron, and sidewalk replacement —
  but BEFORE concrete is poured.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Formwork alignment and dimensions
  - Curb cut width (Village of Great Neck: maximum 18 feet for single-family dwelling)
  - Grade and pitch (must match existing sidewalk and street grade)
  - Base preparation under forms
  - Reinforcement (if specified)
  - ADA-compliant detectable warning surfaces at pedestrian crossings (if applicable)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Demolish and repour curb cut, apron, and affected sidewalk sections
  - Typical cost: $3,000-$8,000
  - May require road opening permit from Nassau County DPW (for county roads) or
    Town of North Hempstead (for town roads) — adding weeks of delay

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Village of Great Neck: No permit issued for curb cut until size and location are
    approved by the Village Superintendent of Public Works
  - Village of Great Neck: Not more than one curb cut permitted for single-family dwelling
    on properties with less than 75 feet of street frontage (exceptions require Board of
    Trustees approval after Superintendent consultation)
  - Nassau County DPW: Construction must comply with 2009 County Standard Specification
    and detail sheets for Civil Engineering and Site Development Construction
  - Separate curb cut permit may be needed from Town of North Hempstead or Nassau County
    DPW depending on road jurisdiction


=============================================================================
SECTION 2: FOUNDATION WORK
=============================================================================

2.1 Footing Inspection — BEFORE Concrete Pour
-----------------------------------------------
WHAT: Footing trenches and reinforcement must be inspected before any concrete is placed.
  This is typically the FIRST major inspection on any project with new foundation work.

WHEN EXACTLY: After footing trenches are fully excavated, all required reinforcing steel
  (rebar) is in place, and any embedded items (anchor bolts, hold-downs) are positioned —
  but BEFORE any concrete is poured.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Trench dimensions (width and depth per approved plans)
  - Soil conditions at bottom of trench (undisturbed bearing soil)
  - Rebar size, spacing, placement, and cover (typically 3" minimum from earth)
  - Rebar lap splices and ties
  - Anchor bolt placement and projection
  - Hold-down bracket embedments
  - Keyway forms (for foundation wall connection)
  - Setback verification from property lines
  - Frost depth compliance (Nassau County: minimum 42 inches from finish grade to
    bottom of footing for exterior footings; 12 inches minimum below undisturbed ground)
  - Footing thickness (typically 12 inches of concrete)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - CANNOT pour concrete — if poured without inspection, building inspector can require
    complete removal and repour of footings
  - Typical cost: $10,000-$30,000+ for demolition and replacement
  - If foundation walls are already on top, ENTIRE foundation may need to come down
  - This is the single most expensive inspection to miss on any project

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - All Great Neck villages require this inspection per NYS code
  - Thomaston explicitly lists "footing/pier" as a required inspection stage
  - Kings Point requires a Foundation Location Certification form
  - Great Neck Estates: if building footprint changed, as-built survey required before CO

REFERENCED CODE: IRC R109.1.1 (Foundation Inspection)
  "Inspection of the foundation shall be made after poles or piers are set or after
  trenches or basement areas are excavated and any required reinforcing steel is in place
  and supported prior to the placing of concrete."

2.2 Foundation Wall Inspection — BEFORE Backfill
--------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Foundation walls must be inspected after construction but before earth is backfilled
  against them.

WHEN EXACTLY: After foundation walls are poured or constructed (block, ICF, etc.),
  waterproofing/damp-proofing is applied, drainage board or tile is installed, and any
  required insulation is placed — but BEFORE backfill begins.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Foundation wall height, thickness, and alignment
  - Anchor bolt placement for sill plates
  - Waterproofing or damp-proofing application (complete coverage, no gaps)
  - Foundation drain tile and filter fabric
  - Window well installation (if applicable)
  - Foundation wall insulation (if exterior insulation strategy)
  - Step-down transitions in foundation walls
  - Penetrations sealed (pipe sleeves, utility entries)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Re-excavation around entire foundation perimeter
  - Typical cost: $5,000-$15,000+ depending on foundation size and depth
  - May damage already-installed drainage tile, waterproofing, or landscaping
  - If waterproofing was defective, water damage may already be occurring

REFERENCED CODE: IRC R109.1.1 notes foundation inspection includes "sheeting and shoring,
  waterproofing, insulation, and location of walls and columns with respect to footings,
  crawl space or basement foundation walls."

2.3 Rebar Inspection (Structural Concrete) — BEFORE Pour
----------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Any structural concrete element requiring reinforcement must have the rebar inspected
  before concrete placement. This applies to footings, foundation walls, retaining walls,
  grade beams, piers, and structural slabs.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all reinforcing steel is placed, tied, supported on chairs or bolsters,
  with proper cover and lap lengths — but BEFORE concrete truck arrives.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Rebar size (bar diameter matches plans)
  - Rebar spacing (on-center dimensions match plans)
  - Rebar cover (minimum distance from form face or earth)
  - Lap splice lengths
  - Hook configurations at terminations
  - Support chairs/bolsters holding rebar at correct elevation
  - Embedded items (anchor bolts, hold-downs, post bases)
  - Conduit or sleeve penetrations

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - If concrete is already poured: no way to inspect rebar without destructive testing
  - Building official may require load testing or core sampling ($1,000-$5,000)
  - In worst case: demolish and repour ($10,000-$50,000+ for large pours)

2.4 Slab-on-Grade Inspection — BEFORE Pour
--------------------------------------------
WHAT: Under-slab preparations including vapor barrier, insulation, plumbing, and
  reinforcement must be inspected before the slab is poured.

WHEN EXACTLY: After the following are complete: sub-slab gravel compacted, vapor barrier
  (typically 6-mil poly) installed, under-slab plumbing roughed in and tested, any
  under-slab insulation placed, wire mesh or rebar positioned — but BEFORE concrete pour.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Sub-slab gravel (type, thickness, compaction)
  - Vapor barrier (coverage, seams sealed, no tears)
  - Under-slab plumbing (supply lines, drains, tested for leaks)
  - Radon mitigation piping (if required)
  - Slab reinforcement (mesh or rebar on chairs)
  - Slab insulation (if required by energy code)
  - Expansion joint material at foundation walls

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Break out and repour slab: $8,000-$25,000+
  - Under-slab plumbing leaks discovered later require saw-cutting slab
  - Vapor barrier issues cause chronic moisture problems

SECTION 3: FRAMING
=============================================================================

3.1 Rough Framing Inspection — BEFORE Insulation and Drywall
--------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All structural framing must be inspected after the rough-in of electrical, plumbing,
  mechanical, and fire protection systems are approved, but before any insulation or wall
  covering is installed. This is the LAST CHANCE to see the entire structural skeleton.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all of the following are complete and approved:
  - All structural framing (walls, floors, roof)
  - Rough electrical approved
  - Rough plumbing approved
  - Rough mechanical (HVAC) approved
  - Fireblocking in place
  - Draft-stopping in place
  - Sheathing nailed (exterior)
  - Hold-downs and hurricane ties installed
  But BEFORE:
  - Insulation is installed
  - Drywall or other wall coverings go up

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All framing members (studs, headers, beams, joists, rafters)
  - Member sizes match approved plans
  - Proper nailing patterns (especially at shear walls)
  - Joist hangers and hardware (Simpson ties, hold-downs, post bases)
  - Fireblocking at floor/ceiling levels, soffits, stairways
  - Draft-stopping in attic spaces at dwelling unit separations
  - Headers over all openings (proper sizing)
  - Bearing points aligned from roof to foundation
  - Bracing (let-in bracing, shear panels, metal strapping)
  - Sheathing nailing at edges and field (especially shear walls)
  - Notches and holes in framing members within allowable limits
  - Stair framing (rise, run, headroom)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove ALL drywall, insulation, and interior finishes to expose framing
  - Typical cost: $5,000-$30,000+ depending on project size
  - Effectively restarts the project from rough stage
  - Most common and most expensive "covered-up" mistake

REFERENCED CODE: IRC R109.1.4 — "Framing and masonry inspection shall be made after the
  roof, masonry, framing, firestopping, draftstopping, and bracing are in place and after
  the plumbing, mechanical, and electrical rough inspections are approved."

3.2 Shear Wall Nailing — Special Inspection BEFORE Covering
-------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: In areas where engineered shear walls are specified, the nailing pattern requires
  either a special inspection or verification during the framing inspection.

WHEN EXACTLY: After structural panel sheathing is nailed to framing, with all fasteners
  installed — but BEFORE any exterior finish (siding, stucco) or interior finish (drywall)
  covers the sheathing.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Sheathing grade and thickness (matches structural plans)
  - Nail or staple size (diameter and length)
  - Nail spacing at panel edges (critical — often 3", 4", or 6" o.c.)
  - Nail spacing in field of panel (typically 12" o.c.)
  - Edge distance (minimum 3/8" from panel edge)
  - Framing member width at adjoining panel edges (minimum 3" for two panels)
  - Hold-down anchors properly installed and connected
  - Blocking between panels where required
  - Number of fastener lines matches plans

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove siding and/or drywall to expose sheathing and nailing
  - If nailing is deficient: add nails or replace sheathing panels
  - Typical cost: $2,000-$10,000+ per wall

REFERENCED CODE: 2020 Building Code of New York State, Chapter 17 (Special Inspections)
  - Periodic special inspection required for nailing, bolting, anchoring of seismic
    force-resisting systems including wood shear walls
  - EXCEPTION: Not required where fastener spacing exceeds 4 inches on center

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Nassau County is in Seismic Design Category A or B (low seismic hazard)
  - Most residential projects will NOT require special structural inspections unless
    the engineer specifically calls for them
  - However, the building inspector will verify shear wall nailing during the standard
    framing inspection

SECTION 4: PLUMBING
=============================================================================

4.1 Underground Plumbing Inspection — BEFORE Backfill or Slab Pour
--------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All underground drain, waste, and vent (DWV) piping and water supply lines must
  be inspected before they are buried under a slab or backfilled with earth.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all underground piping is installed, connected, and supported in
  properly bedded trenches — but BEFORE any backfill or concrete slab is placed over them.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All underground piping (DWV and supply)
  - Pipe material, size, and slope (minimum 1/4" per foot for 3" and smaller drains;
    1/8" per foot for 4" and larger)
  - Proper bedding material under pipes
  - Joint connections
  - Cleanout locations
  - Test results: water test (10-foot head of water held for 15 minutes) or air test

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Saw-cut concrete slab to expose piping: $2,000-$5,000+ per section
  - Re-excavate trenches if in earth: $1,000-$3,000
  - If leak discovered after concealment: extensive water damage, mold risk

REFERENCED CODE: NYS Residential Code Section P2503 — "Underground inspection and testing
  shall be made after trenches or ditches are excavated and bedded, piping installed, and
  before any backfill is put in place."

  CRITICAL: "Any trench backfilled without completed inspection shall be re-excavated to
  the degree necessary as determined by the Inspector."

4.2 Rough Plumbing Inspection — BEFORE Walls Close
-----------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All above-ground supply, DWV, and gas piping must be inspected while still exposed
  in framed walls and ceilings.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all rough plumbing is installed (supply lines, DWV piping through walls
  and ceilings, venting through roof), and pressure testing is complete — but BEFORE any
  insulation, drywall, or other wall covering is installed.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All supply piping (material, size, support, no contact with incompatible metals)
  - All DWV piping (material, size, slope, proper venting)
  - Vent terminations through roof
  - Proper support (hangers, strapping at required intervals)
  - Pipe protection (nail plates where piping passes through framing within 1.25" of edge)
  - Water hammer arrestors
  - Shut-off valve locations
  - Test results: water supply test (working pressure for minimum 15 minutes)
  - DWV test (water test: 10-foot head above highest fitting for 15 minutes, must be
    visually leak-free)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove drywall to expose piping: $2,000-$10,000+ depending on extent
  - If leak discovered after walls close: water damage to drywall, insulation, flooring
  - Mold remediation costs if water intrusion occurs undetected

4.3 Sewer Connection Inspection — BEFORE Backfill
---------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The building sewer (pipe from house to public sewer main) must be inspected before
  the trench is backfilled.

WHEN EXACTLY: After the sewer lateral is connected from the building to the public sewer
  main, properly bedded, and tested — but BEFORE the trench is filled.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Pipe material, size, and slope
  - Connection to public sewer (proper fitting, no offset)
  - Bedding material
  - Test plug at point of connection with public sewer
  - Water test: fill building sewer with water, not less than 10-foot head, maintain
    pressure for 15 minutes, must be watertight
  - Backwater valve (if required)
  - Cleanout at property line

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Re-excavate trench (may be under driveway, landscaping, or street)
  - If under public road: road opening permit required, traffic control, road restoration
  - Typical cost: $3,000-$15,000+ depending on depth and location
  - May need photographic/video verification as alternative to re-excavation

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Great Neck Estates requires sewer district compliance certificate (Great Neck Sewer
    District or Belgrave Sewer District) before CO issuance
  - Nassau County DPW inspects connections to county sewer infrastructure
  - All sewer connections must be inspected by Department Inspector unless photographic
    or video verification is accepted

SECTION 5: ELECTRICAL
=============================================================================

5.1 Rough Electrical Inspection — BEFORE Walls Close
------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All electrical wiring, boxes, conduit, and panel connections must be inspected while
  still exposed in framed walls, ceilings, and floors.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all rough wiring is installed — branch circuits, switch legs, home runs
  to panel, boxes mounted, grounding conductors run, AFCI/GFCI requirements identified —
  but BEFORE any insulation, drywall, or wall covering is installed.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All branch circuit wiring (proper gauge for circuit amperage)
  - Box fill calculations (not overfilled)
  - Box mounting (proper height, securely fastened)
  - Grounding system (equipment grounding conductors in all circuits)
  - Service entrance conductors
  - Panel location and mounting
  - Proper wire protection (nail plates where wire passes through framing within 1.25"
    of edge; conduit where exposed or required)
  - Proper support (staples/straps within 12" of boxes and at required intervals)
  - Separation of low-voltage and high-voltage wiring
  - Smoke/CO detector locations and wiring (interconnected, battery backup)
  - Arc-fault circuit interrupter (AFCI) protection for bedrooms and other required areas
  - Ground-fault circuit interrupter (GFCI) protection identification for kitchens,
    bathrooms, garages, outdoors, basements, laundry

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove drywall to expose wiring: $2,000-$10,000+
  - If wiring deficiencies found after walls close: extensive demolition required
  - Fire hazard risk if defective wiring concealed without inspection
  - Insurance may not cover fire loss if unpermitted/uninspected electrical work

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - All electrical work must be performed by a licensed electrician
  - Thomaston explicitly lists "rough electrical" and "electrical service" as separate
    required inspections
  - Kings Point lists "electrical" as a required inspection stage
  - Great Neck Plaza requires electrical permits separate from building permits

5.2 Electrical Panel Inspection
---------------------------------
WHAT: The main electrical panel (and any sub-panels) must be inspected for proper
  installation, circuit identification, and code compliance.

WHEN EXACTLY: Can occur during rough inspection (panel box and feed wiring) and during
  final inspection (completed panel with all breakers, labeling, and covers).

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Panel rating matches service size
  - Proper working clearance (30" wide, 36" deep, 78" high in front of panel)
  - Circuit directory complete and accurate
  - All breakers properly rated for wire gauge
  - AFCI breakers installed where required
  - GFCI breakers installed where required
  - Neutral/ground bus bars separated in main panel
  - Proper bonding
  - Service disconnect accessible

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Panel itself is usually accessible (not concealed behind drywall)
  - However, if feeders running through walls are wrong, drywall removal may be needed
  - Fire marshal may issue violation if panel does not meet current code

SECTION 6: ROOFING
=============================================================================

6.1 Roof Sheathing Inspection — BEFORE Underlayment/Shingles
--------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: When roof sheathing (plywood or OSB) is replaced or installed new, it should be
  inspected before being covered with underlayment and shingles.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all roof sheathing is installed and nailed to rafters/trusses — but
  BEFORE underlayment (felt paper or synthetic) and roofing material (shingles, etc.)
  are applied.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Sheathing grade and thickness (matches plans; typically 7/16" or 15/32" OSB or
    plywood for residential)
  - Nailing pattern (typically 6" o.c. at edges, 12" o.c. in field, per Table R602.3(1))
  - H-clips between panels (if required for span)
  - Panel edge spacing (1/8" gap for expansion)
  - No damage, delamination, or water staining
  - Proper bearing on rafters at all edges
  - Roof ventilation openings (ridge vent, soffit vents) properly framed

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - May not require full tear-off if building inspector accepts visual/photographic
    documentation
  - If sheathing is deficient: tear off all roofing materials to replace sheathing
  - Typical cost: $5,000-$20,000+ for re-roofing

NOTE: Many jurisdictions, including Great Neck area villages, handle re-roofing with a
  single final inspection rather than requiring a sheathing inspection — UNLESS structural
  sheathing is being replaced. Always confirm with your village building department whether
  a mid-work sheathing inspection is required for your specific project.

6.2 Underlayment and Ice/Water Shield — BEFORE Shingles
---------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Underlayment installation (especially ice and water shield in cold climate areas)
  should be verified before final roofing material covers it.

WHEN EXACTLY: After underlayment (and ice barrier, if required) is installed over sheathing —
  but BEFORE shingles or other finish roofing is applied.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Underlayment type and coverage
  - Ice barrier extending minimum 24 inches past exterior wall line (NYS Residential Code
    R905.1.2 — required in areas with history of ice damming, which includes all of
    Nassau County / Long Island)
  - Proper overlap of underlayment courses
  - Drip edge installed: under underlayment at eaves, over underlayment at rakes
  - Valley treatment (ice barrier in valleys)
  - Flashing at roof-to-wall intersections

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - If ice barrier is missing or insufficient: ice dam water intrusion causing interior
    water damage, mold, and structural deterioration
  - Fix requires removing shingles in affected areas
  - Typical cost: $2,000-$8,000 per affected area

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Nassau County / Long Island IS in a cold climate zone with ice dam risk
  - Ice barrier is REQUIRED per NYS Residential Code R905.1.2
  - Great Neck area villages typically require a roofing/siding permit
  - Inspection approach varies: some villages do a single final inspection, others may
    require mid-work inspection for structural changes


=============================================================================
SECTION 7: HVAC / MECHANICAL
=============================================================================

7.1 Rough Mechanical (HVAC) Inspection — BEFORE Walls Close
--------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All heating, ventilation, air conditioning ductwork, refrigerant lines, gas piping,
  and exhaust systems must be inspected while still exposed in framed walls and ceilings.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all ductwork, refrigerant lines, gas piping, exhaust ducts, and
  combustion air provisions are installed — but BEFORE insulation or drywall covers them.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All ductwork (material, size, connections, sealing)
  - Duct support and fastening
  - Duct insulation (where required — typically supply ducts in unconditioned spaces)
  - Refrigerant line routing and insulation
  - Gas piping (material, size, support, shut-off valves, drip legs)
  - Exhaust duct routing (bathroom fans to exterior, not into attic)
  - Kitchen exhaust duct routing (to exterior)
  - Dryer vent routing (to exterior, maximum duct length with deductions for elbows)
  - Combustion air openings for fuel-burning appliances
  - Clearances from combustibles for flue pipes and chimneys
  - Return air pathways

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove drywall/ceiling to expose ductwork: $2,000-$10,000+
  - Improperly vented exhaust (especially into attic) causes mold and structural damage
  - Gas piping leaks are a safety hazard — reinspection is mandatory
  - Improperly routed dryer vents are a fire hazard

REFERENCED CODE: IRC R109.1.2 — "Inspection of the plumbing, mechanical, gas and
  electrical systems shall be made after rough installation of such systems and prior
  to covering or concealment."

7.2 Gas Piping Pressure Test — BEFORE Concealment
---------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Gas piping must be pressure tested before it is enclosed in walls or underground.

WHEN EXACTLY: After gas piping is completely installed but before concealment. Testing
  typically occurs as part of the rough mechanical inspection.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All gas piping joints and connections
  - Test gauge showing proper pressure (typically 3 psi for 10 minutes for low-pressure
    residential systems)
  - Shut-off valve at each appliance
  - Drip legs (sediment traps) at each appliance connection
  - Proper pipe support

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Gas leaks behind walls are extremely dangerous (explosion/asphyxiation risk)
  - Must open walls to access and test piping
  - Gas company may refuse to turn on service without approved inspection


=============================================================================
SECTION 8: DECK AND PATIO CONSTRUCTION
=============================================================================

8.1 Deck Footing/Post Hole Inspection — BEFORE Concrete Pour
---------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Deck footing holes must be inspected before concrete is poured into them.

WHEN EXACTLY: After post holes are dug to required depth, any rebar is placed, and
  post brackets/anchors are ready — but BEFORE concrete is poured.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Hole depth: minimum 42 inches from finish grade (NYS frost line requirement);
    some areas 48 inches — verify with local building department
  - Hole diameter matches plans (typically 12"-24" depending on load)
  - Soil conditions at bottom (undisturbed native soil capable of bearing load)
  - Rebar placement (if specified in plans)
  - Post anchor/bracket positioning
  - Setback from property lines verified
  - Number and location of footings matches approved plans

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Dig out concrete-filled post holes and repour: $500-$2,000+ per footing
  - If deck framing is already up: may need to shore up or disassemble deck
  - If frost depth is inadequate: footing will heave in winter, causing deck
    structural failure

REFERENCED CODE: NYS Residential Code R507.3 — Exterior footings shall be placed
  minimum 12 inches below undisturbed ground surface, and below frost line (42" in
  Nassau County area).

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Great Neck area frost line: 42 inches minimum
  - All Great Neck villages require deck permits with structural details
  - Thomaston lists "footing/pier" as explicit inspection stage

8.2 Ledger Board Attachment Inspection — BEFORE Concealment
-------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The ledger board (where deck attaches to house) must be inspected for proper
  attachment and flashing.

WHEN EXACTLY: After the ledger board is bolted/lagged to the house framing and flashing
  is installed — but BEFORE any decking or fascia conceals the connection.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Ledger board attachment method (lag screws or through-bolts — NOT nails)
  - Bolt/lag size, spacing, and stagger pattern (per IRC Table R507.5)
  - Flashing installed per R703.4 to prevent water from contacting house band joist
  - Spacer or standoff (if required for drainage behind ledger)
  - Ledger fastened to house band joist or rim joist (NOT just to siding or sheathing)
  - No siding trapped behind ledger

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - If connection is inadequate: deck can separate from house (catastrophic collapse risk —
    this is the #1 cause of deck failures)
  - Removal of decking to expose ledger connection: $1,000-$3,000+
  - If water infiltration occurs behind improperly flashed ledger: rot damage to house
    structure requiring extensive repair

CRITICAL SAFETY NOTE: Per NYS Residential Code, "Where positive connection to the primary
  building structure cannot be verified during inspection, decks shall be self-supporting."
  This means freestanding posts at the house end — a much more expensive solution.

8.3 Deck Framing Inspection — BEFORE Decking Installed
--------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All deck structural framing must be inspected before the deck boards cover them.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all posts, beams, joists, and hardware are installed — but BEFORE
  decking boards, railing posts, and stair stringers are covered.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Post-to-beam connections (proper hardware)
  - Beam size and span (matches plans)
  - Joist size, spacing, and span (matches plans)
  - Joist hangers at all joist-to-beam and joist-to-ledger connections
  - Blocking/bridging (if required)
  - Lateral bracing
  - Hardware (galvanized or stainless for treated lumber)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove decking to expose framing: $1,000-$5,000+
  - Structural deficiencies may require partial rebuild

SECTION 9: FENCING
=============================================================================

9.1 Fence Post Hole Depth Inspection — BEFORE Concrete Fill
--------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Post hole depth should be verified before concrete is poured to set fence posts.

WHEN EXACTLY: After post holes are dug — but BEFORE concrete or gravel is placed and
  posts are set.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Post hole depth (typically 1/3 of total post length underground, or per engineering;
    common residential: 24"-36" deep for 6-foot fence)
  - Post hole diameter (typically 3x post width; 10"-12" for 4x4 posts)
  - Post spacing (typically 6-8 feet on center)
  - Location relative to property lines (fence typically must be entirely on owner's
    property; verify exact setback requirement with village)
  - Soil conditions

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Dig out post footings and reset: $100-$300 per post
  - For a typical fence: $1,000-$5,000+ to redo all posts
  - If fence is in wrong location (over property line): must be moved entirely

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Most Great Neck villages require fence permits
  - Typical height limits vary by village and yard location:
    Front yard: max 4 feet; Side yard: max 5 feet; Rear yard: max 6 feet
  - Some villages require a survey showing fence location relative to property lines
  - Village of Great Neck and Great Neck Estates have specific fence codes
  - NOTE: Some Great Neck villages handle fence post inspections with a single final
    inspection rather than a separate post-hole inspection. Confirm with your village
    building department whether they want to see post holes before concrete.

IMPORTANT: Many villages in the Great Neck area do NOT require a separate post-hole
  inspection for residential fences — they do a single final inspection. However, if your
  fence is on or near a property line, getting a survey and documenting post hole depths
  with photos (even if not formally inspected) is strongly recommended to avoid disputes.

SECTION 10: SWIMMING POOLS
=============================================================================

10.1 Steel/Rebar Inspection — BEFORE Gunite/Shotcrete
-------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The steel reinforcement cage for an in-ground concrete (gunite/shotcrete) pool must
  be inspected before the concrete shell is sprayed.

WHEN EXACTLY: After the pool is excavated, steel rebar cage is fully tied, all embedded
  items (returns, skimmer, main drain, light niches) are positioned, bonding wire is
  attached to rebar, and plumbing is pressure-tested — but BEFORE gunite or shotcrete
  is applied.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Rebar size and spacing (typically #3 or #4 rebar at 12" on center both ways for
    residential pools, per engineer's design)
  - Rebar cover (minimum distance from earth face and from finished pool surface)
  - Bond attachments (rebar must be bonded per NEC 680.26(B)(1)(b) — the entire
    concrete pool shell is considered conductive)
  - Bonding wire (solid 8 AWG bare copper conductor or structural rebar can serve
    as bonding grid per NEC)
  - Bond wire spacing and connections
  - All embedded plumbing fittings (returns, skimmer, main drain, suction lines)
  - Light niche placement
  - Plumbing pressure test results (piping must pass pressure test before being
    encased in concrete)
  - Plumbing routing: pipes should be in trenches around the pool perimeter, NOT
    embedded in the wall (perpendicular penetrations only — pipe in the wall creates
    a thin structural section prone to cracking)

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - CANNOT inspect rebar once gunite/shotcrete is applied
  - If rebar is deficient: entire pool shell may need to be demolished and rebuilt
  - Typical cost: $20,000-$50,000+ for pool demolition and rebuild
  - Bonding deficiency: electrocution risk — mandatory correction
  - This is THE most critical and expensive pool inspection to miss

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Village of Great Neck: Swimming pools permitted on noncommercial basis when
    approved by Board of Trustees after public hearing
  - Great Neck Estates: pool cannot exceed 25% of unoccupied rear yard area;
    minimum 20 feet from any lot line
  - Kings Point: swimming pool permit fee $1,500; requires pool location survey
  - All villages require compliance with NYS Residential Code R326 (Swimming Pools,
    Spas and Hot Tubs)
  - Electrical inspection by certified electrical inspection agency approved by village

10.2 Pool Plumbing Inspection — BEFORE Backfill
--------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All pool plumbing (supply and return lines, main drain, skimmer connections, filter
  connections) must be inspected before trenches are backfilled.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all pool plumbing is installed in trenches around the pool perimeter
  and connected to the equipment pad — but BEFORE trenches are backfilled with earth.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - All piping (material, typically Schedule 40 PVC for residential pools)
  - Pipe size (suction lines, return lines, skimmer lines per design)
  - Pipe routing (in trenches around perimeter, not embedded in pool wall)
  - Glued joints (properly cemented, no dry fits)
  - Pressure test (all lines must hold pressure — typically 20 psi for 24 hours)
  - Backflow prevention / vacuum breakers
  - Main drain compliance (dual main drains per Virginia Graeme Baker Act)
  - Equipment connections

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Re-excavate trenches around pool: $2,000-$5,000
  - If plumbing leaks after backfill: undermining of pool shell, deck settlement,
    sinkholes in yard
  - Leak detection after concealment: $500-$2,000 for electronic leak detection
  - Repair of concealed leak: $1,000-$5,000 per leak location

10.3 Pool Electrical Inspection — BEFORE Concrete Deck
---------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All pool electrical systems (bonding, lighting, equipment wiring) must be inspected.

WHEN EXACTLY: After all electrical conduit, bonding devices for the reinforcement, niche
  and lighting fixtures are installed — but BEFORE any concrete cover or deck is placed.
  There is typically also a final electrical inspection after equipment is connected.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Equipotential bonding grid (all metallic parts within 5 feet horizontally of pool
    edge and 12 feet vertically of water level must be bonded)
  - Bonding connections to pool steel, metal fence, ladder, handrails, diving board
    hardware, light niches, equipment
  - GFCI protection for all pool equipment circuits
  - Wiring method and burial depth for underground feeders
  - Light fixture installation (proper niche, watertight, wet-niche fixtures rated
    for submersion)
  - Disconnect switch within sight of equipment
  - Equipment grounding

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Bonding deficiencies = electrocution risk — this is a life-safety inspection
  - Break out concrete deck to access bonding connections: $3,000-$10,000+
  - Electrical permit revocation if work concealed without inspection

REFERENCED CODE: NEC Article 680 (Swimming Pools, Fountains, and Similar Installations)

10.4 Pool Barrier/Fence Inspection — BEFORE Use
--------------------------------------------------
WHAT: The safety barrier (fence) around the pool must be inspected before the pool is
  filled and used.

WHEN EXACTLY: After permanent barrier (fence) is installed — but BEFORE pool is filled
  for use. Note: NYS code requires a temporary barrier (minimum 48 inches high) during
  construction, replaced with permanent barrier within 90 days.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Fence height: minimum 48 inches (4 feet), maximum 6 feet (per local code)
  - Self-closing gate with self-latching mechanism
  - Latch at least 54 inches above grade (or on pool side if lower)
  - No openings allowing passage of a 4-inch sphere
  - No climbable features (horizontal rails on outside)
  - Gate opens outward (away from pool)
  - Covers complete pool area

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Cannot fill or use pool until barrier passes inspection
  - Non-compliant barrier: liability exposure for drowning/injury
  - Village can issue stop-use order
  - Insurance may not cover incidents without compliant barrier

SECTION 11: ADDITIONS AND EXTENSIONS
=============================================================================

An addition or extension to an existing home requires the FULL sequence of inspections
for each construction phase. Each phase must be inspected before the next phase conceals it.

11.1 Complete Inspection Sequence for Home Additions
-----------------------------------------------------

PHASE 1 — FOUNDATION (must be inspected before Phase 2 begins)
  1a. Footing excavation: soil conditions, dimensions, rebar — BEFORE concrete pour
  1b. Footing pour: reinforcement in place — BEFORE concrete placement
  1c. Foundation walls: formwork, rebar, anchor bolts — BEFORE wall pour
  1d. Waterproofing: damp-proofing/waterproofing applied — BEFORE backfill
  1e. Under-slab: plumbing, vapor barrier, gravel — BEFORE slab pour (if slab-on-grade)

PHASE 2 — FRAMING (must be inspected before Phase 3 begins)
  2a. Structural framing complete: walls, floors, roof structure
  2b. Sheathing and nailing verified
  2c. Connection to existing structure verified (proper tie-in, no structural compromise)
  2d. Fireblocking and draft-stopping in place
  2e. Weather barrier (house wrap) installed

PHASE 3 — ROUGH MECHANICAL/ELECTRICAL/PLUMBING (must be inspected before Phase 4)
  3a. Rough plumbing: supply and DWV piping — BEFORE walls close
  3b. Rough electrical: all wiring and boxes — BEFORE walls close
  3c. Rough HVAC: ductwork, refrigerant lines, gas piping — BEFORE walls close
  3d. Rough fire protection (if sprinklers required) — BEFORE walls close

PHASE 4 — INSULATION AND ENERGY CODE (must be inspected before Phase 5)
  4a. Insulation inspection: R-values, coverage, continuity — BEFORE drywall
  4b. Air sealing verification: penetrations sealed, gaps filled — BEFORE drywall
  4c. Vapor barrier placement (if required) — BEFORE drywall
  4d. Energy code documentation: insulation certificates from installer with R-value,
      type, manufacturer, coverage area, and (for blown/sprayed) installed thickness,
      settled thickness, density, and number of bags

PHASE 5 — WALL COVERINGS AND FINISHES
  5a. Drywall/lath inspection (if fire-rated assembly): fastener spacing, joint treatment
  5b. Interior finishes proceed

PHASE 6 — FINAL INSPECTIONS
  6a. Final plumbing: fixtures, testing
  6b. Final electrical: devices, panels, testing (GFCI/AFCI)
  6c. Final mechanical: equipment, ventilation
  6d. Final building: overall completion, egress, safety, accessibility
  6e. Certificate of Occupancy / Certificate of Completion issued

CONSEQUENCES OF SKIPPING ANY PHASE:
  - Every subsequent phase covers the previous phase's work
  - Missing Phase 1 (foundation) before Phase 2 (framing): must remove framing to
    inspect foundation ($10,000-$50,000+)
  - Missing Phase 3 (rough-ins) before Phase 4 (insulation): must remove insulation
    ($1,000-$3,000) — least expensive recovery
  - Missing Phase 4 (insulation) before Phase 5 (drywall): must remove drywall
    ($2,000-$10,000+)
  - Missing any phase: CO WILL NOT be issued. Period.

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Great Neck Estates: additions/alterations exceeding $3,000 or 2,000 cubic feet are
    subject to ALL code provisions
  - Great Neck Estates: for structures >2,000 sq ft total floor area, architect/engineer
    must file final certificate certifying conformity with filed plans
  - Great Neck Estates: cash deposit of $25,000 required where construction exceeds 40%
    of floor area ratio (site restoration guarantee)
  - Kensington: ALL applications reviewed by Architectural Review Board (ARB)
  - Kings Point: permits valid for 24 months; extension fees after expiration
  - Kings Point: Foundation Location Certification and Building Height Certification
    forms required
  - Thomaston: Landmark Preservation Commission review for designated properties

SECTION 12: INSULATION AND ENERGY CODE INSPECTION
=============================================================================

12.1 Thermal Envelope Inspection — BEFORE Drywall
---------------------------------------------------
WHAT: All insulation, air sealing, and vapor barrier components of the building thermal
  envelope must be inspected before any wall or ceiling covering is installed.

WHEN EXACTLY: After insulation is installed in all exterior walls, ceilings, floors over
  unconditioned spaces, and any other thermal envelope components — but BEFORE drywall,
  paneling, or any other covering is installed over them.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Insulation type and R-value (manufacturer's R-value mark must be readable)
  - R-value compliance with NYS Energy Code:
    - Nassau County is Climate Zone 4A
    - Minimum R-values (2020 ECCCNYS prescriptive path):
      Ceiling: R-49; Wood frame wall: R-20 or R-13+5ci; Floor: R-19;
      Basement wall: R-15ci or R-19; Crawl space wall: R-15ci or R-19
  - Insulation installed continuously without gaps, voids, compressions, or misalignment
  - Proper contact with air barrier on all six sides of each cavity
  - Air sealing at all penetrations (wiring, plumbing, duct, recessed lights)
  - Vapor barrier location and type (if required)
  - Installer certification listing type, manufacturer, R-value, coverage area, and
    (for blown-in or sprayed): initial installed thickness, settled thickness, settled
    R-value, installed density, number of bags

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - Remove drywall to expose insulation: $2,000-$10,000+
  - Energy code non-compliance: CO will not be issued
  - If insulation is wrong R-value or has gaps: energy performance suffers,
    comfort complaints, potential condensation/mold issues

REFERENCED CODE: NYS Energy Conservation Construction Code; IRC R109.1.5 —
  "Inspection of insulation envelope requirements shall be made after the installation
  of the building thermal envelope insulation and before covering same with any other
  materials."

NASSAU COUNTY / GREAT NECK SPECIFICS:
  - Effective December 31, 2025: new 2025 NYS Energy Code applies to building permit
    applications submitted after this date (may have stricter R-value requirements)
  - All Great Neck area villages enforce NYS Energy Code
  - Energy code compliance documentation is required for CO issuance

SECTION 13: FIRE-RESISTANCE-RATED CONSTRUCTION
=============================================================================

13.1 Fire-Rated Assembly Inspection — BEFORE Final Covering
-------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT: Fire-resistance-rated walls, floors, and ceilings (e.g., dwelling unit separation
  walls in two-family homes, walls at property-line adjacencies) must be inspected during
  installation.

WHEN EXACTLY: After the fire-rated assembly covering material (typically Type X drywall)
  is installed with proper fastener spacing — but BEFORE joint treatment (taping/mudding)
  and finish coats are applied. The intent is to verify that the correct material is used
  and properly fastened.

WHAT MUST BE VISIBLE/ACCESSIBLE:
  - Drywall type (5/8" Type X for most fire-rated assemblies)
  - Number of layers (1-hour walls typically require one layer each side; 2-hour may
    require two layers)
  - Fastener type, size, and spacing (per assembly listing)
  - Fireblocking at all required locations
  - Fire-stopping at penetrations (pipes, wires, ducts through fire-rated assemblies)
  - No gaps at edges or joints
  - Proper edge support at all drywall edges

CONSEQUENCES IF MISSED:
  - If wrong drywall type or insufficient layers: strip and replace
  - Fire-stopping deficiencies at penetrations: must open wall to access and correct
  - Life-safety issue: fire-rated separation is critical for occupant protection
  - Fire marshal may issue violation

REFERENCED CODE: IRC R109.1.5.1 — Fire-resistance-rated construction inspection applies
  to "fire-resistance-rated construction required at dwelling unit separations and at
  exterior walls adjacent to a property line."

SECTION 14: GENERAL CONSEQUENCES OF MISSING INSPECTIONS
=============================================================================

14.1 Stop Work Orders
-----------------------
The building inspector can issue a stop work order at any time if work is proceeding
without required inspections. ALL work must halt until the violation is corrected.
Continuing work after a stop work order can result in additional fines and permit
revocation.

14.2 Mandatory Exposure of Concealed Work
-------------------------------------------
Per NYS code and all Great Neck village codes, if work is covered without inspection,
the building official can require that the work be uncovered for inspection. The cost of
uncovering and restoring is borne entirely by the property owner or contractor — NOT the
village.

Quote from NYS Plumbing Code: "Any trench backfilled without completed inspection shall
be re-excavated to the degree necessary as determined by the Inspector."

14.3 Denial of Certificate of Occupancy
-----------------------------------------
A Certificate of Occupancy (CO) or Certificate of Completion (CC) CANNOT be issued until
ALL required inspections have been completed and approved. Without a CO:
  - The structure cannot be legally occupied
  - Insurance coverage may be void or limited
  - Property cannot be sold or refinanced (title search reveals open permits)
  - Village can issue violations for occupying without CO

14.4 Fines and Penalties
--------------------------
Under NYS law, performing construction work without required permits or failing to obtain
required inspections can result in:
  - Civil penalties (fines per day of violation)
  - Criminal penalties for willful violations
  - Insurance invalidation (if accident occurs on uninspected work)
  - Personal liability for property owners and contractors

14.5 Property Title Issues
----------------------------
Open building permits and uninspected work show up on property title searches. This can:
  - Block property sales
  - Block refinancing
  - Reduce property value
  - Require retroactive compliance (opening walls, excavating) years later

14.6 Cost Summary: What It Costs to "Undo" Covered Work
----------------------------------------------------------
These are approximate ranges for Great Neck area (Nassau County pricing):

  Inspection Missed               | Cost to Expose and Re-inspect
  --------------------------------|-------------------------------
  Footing/rebar before pour       | $10,000 - $50,000+

  Under-slab plumbing             | $2,000 - $5,000 per section
  Slab-on-grade before pour       | $8,000 - $25,000
  Sewer connection before backfill| $3,000 - $15,000
  Rough plumbing in walls         | $2,000 - $10,000
  Rough electrical in walls       | $2,000 - $10,000
  Rough HVAC in walls/ceilings    | $2,000 - $10,000
  Framing before drywall          | $5,000 - $30,000
  Shear wall nailing              | $2,000 - $10,000 per wall
  Insulation before drywall       | $2,000 - $10,000
  Roof sheathing before roofing   | $5,000 - $20,000
  Pool rebar before gunite        | $20,000 - $50,000+
  Pool plumbing before backfill   | $2,000 - $5,000
  Pool bonding before deck        | $3,000 - $10,000

  Drywell before backfill         | $2,000 - $5,000
  Driveway subgrade before paving | $3,000 - $10,000

SECTION 15: SCHEDULING BEST PRACTICES FOR GREAT NECK AREA
=============================================================================

15.1 Notification Requirements
-------------------------------
  - All Great Neck area villages require minimum 24-hour advance notice for inspections
  - Some villages specify 48-hour notice
  - Inspections are subject to scheduling availability
  - Village of Great Neck inspection hours: Monday-Friday, 8 AM - 3 PM
  - Kings Point hours: Monday-Friday, 9:30 AM - 4:30 PM
  - Other villages generally operate 9 AM - 4 PM weekdays

15.2 Sequencing Tips
----------------------
  - Schedule rough plumbing, rough electrical, and rough HVAC inspections BEFORE calling
    for the framing inspection (framing inspection requires all rough-ins to be approved)
  - Schedule the insulation inspection immediately after framing approval — do not start
    drywall until insulation is approved
  - For pool projects, schedule steel/rebar inspection BEFORE the gunite crew arrives —
    gunite crews typically schedule weeks in advance and delays are costly
  - For foundation work, have the inspector verify footings the same day as the pour if
    possible — concrete trucks run on tight schedules
  - Keep the building inspector's direct phone number handy for same-day scheduling needs

15.3 Shared Building Inspectors
---------------------------------
Several Great Neck area villages share building inspectors:
  - Michael Sweeney serves Great Neck and Great Neck Estates
  - Mark Vincent Kruse serves Kensington and Thomaston
This means inspection scheduling may be affected by the inspector's schedule across
multiple villages. Plan ahead and request inspections early.

15.4 Documentation
--------------------
Even if not required by the village, take dated photographs at every inspection stage:
  - Footing trenches with rebar (include tape measure for scale)
  - Foundation walls before backfill
  - All rough-in work (plumbing, electrical, HVAC) before walls close
  - Insulation before drywall
  - Pool steel before gunite
  - Underground work before backfill
This creates a permanent record that can resolve disputes, support insurance claims,
and demonstrate due diligence.
